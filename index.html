Forty-five storefronts have gone dark
in Seattle since the year the streets emptied.
Twenty-five more are on the critical list.

[ The premise ]

This is not a comprehensive census. It is a record kept by hand — a private inventory of the bars, drugstores, dive joints, department stores and dessert shops that quietly stopped existing between Lake Union and Pioneer Square.

[ The shape of it ]

Downtown ground-floor retail vacancy stands near 26%. Worker foot traffic remains at roughly 60% of pre-pandemic baseline. Reported shoplifting incidents are up 22% year-over-year. The map of pain is uneven. The 3rd Avenue corridor and Westlake have absorbed the heaviest losses.

[ How to read this ]

Below: a chronological index of every recorded closure, then a watch list of stores rumored, announced, or in bankruptcy. Then a stylised geography of where the city is losing the most. Then five field notes on what the data says aloud.

    § IV

    Field Notes.
    Five patterns the data keeps repeating.

    i.

    The 3rd Avenue collapse.

    More storefronts have gone dark on this single corridor than in any other part of Seattle. Office vacancy near 30% and the persistent shift to remote work have hollowed out a retail base built around a 9-to-5 city.

    ii.

    The end of Bartell Drugs.

    Eight Seattle locations of the city's beloved local pharmacy chain have closed in the wake of Rite Aid's bankruptcy and CVS's takeover. What had been a reliable corner store on dozens of blocks is, increasingly, a vacant pane of glass.

    iii.

    Anchors weighed down.

    Westlake Center and Pacific Place have lost Macy's, Saks Off Fifth, Forever 21, Barnes & Noble, the Disney Store and a Southeast Asian food hall in six years. The American mall-anchor model has all but vanished from downtown Seattle.

    iv.

    Capitol Hill turns over.

    R Place, The Cuff, Re-bar, B&O Espresso, Glo's, Twice Sold Tales. Legacy LGBTQ+ venues and thirty-year institutions have exited as rents reset and operators retired.

    v.

    Theft as the cited reason.

    Walgreens, Bartell, Target and REI have all named organised retail theft in their public statements. The Seattle Police Department reports shoplifting up 22% year-over-year through mid-2024.
